Notes and Legend
----------------
Other popular versions of Heman Dunh are by Pierre Bensusan and Alan Stivell.
There is a discussion of the origin of this piece on the Mandolin Cafe forum.
https://www.mandolincafe.com/forum/threads/34267-Heman-dubh

The tempo of the MIDI is 120 bpm (quarter notes).

measures 28-30,36-38 - the sheet has E (4th string) in 1st chord as a half note
measure 48 - in the sheet the harmonic on the 2nd string is a half note - this
             can be achieved by playing the following G on the 5th string
measure 50 - in the sheet the upper notes on the 4th beat (B A F# D) are all
             played with the index finger

===   - notes held, length of note, duration
>     - accented, marked, stressed
> > > - decrescendo - becoming gradually softer
bpm   - beats per minute
F.    - Fermata - note(s) held, (long) pause
g     - grace note (ornament) - extra note(s) played immediately before (or
        at the same time as) the main note, then immediately released - the
        following note is usually played as a hammer-on, pull-off, or slide
h     - hammer-on
L.V.  - laissez vibrer (let vibrate) - let the notes continue to ring
M     - ornament - upper mordent - play the main note, followed by the (scale)
        note above, and then return to the original note (may be extended)
p     - (between the notes) pull-off
Tamb. - Tambora (Tambour) - the strings are played all at once by hitting them
        near the bridge with a flat part of the hand (usually with the side of
        the outstretched thumb)
<7>   - natural harmonic on the 7th fret (open string)
<12>  - natural (octave) harmonic on the 12th fret (open string)
